# Conflict & Pushback — Sunny Kolattukudy

Raw material for identity writing. Use when a piece involves disagreement, pushback, hard conversations, or how he handles friction. Pull specifics, don't summarize.

---

## Disagreeing Up

His threshold for escalating: when harm to people — team, customers, organization — is clear and tangible, he'll escalate as high as his voice will reach. For ethical violations (dishonesty, lack of transparency, actual harm), he'll fight hard regardless of personal cost. For bad business direction or wasted effort, he'll look one level above his manager, and if that doesn't land, he'll quietly start looking for another job.

When he can't even get in the room to make the case: that's the deal-breaker. Disagree and commit is fine — being shut out before he can argue is not. He's left companies over this. Not impulsively — he commits, follows through, and leaves cleanly. But he won't stay somewhere that expects him to follow orders without thinking.

Real example: was told by his boss that leadership had decided to keep the legacy codebase rather than go greenfield. He didn't get a chance to make the case. His position: compounding legacy complexity eventually makes the system incomprehensible to everyone, including the product owners who agreed to it. That's exactly what happened. His boss knows he was right. He left.

## Disagreeing Laterally

He doesn't lead with "you're wrong." He leads with questions that make them find it themselves:

- "What happens when...?"
- "What if X goes wrong...?"

If they dismiss the concern — "that should never happen" — he escalates: "Why would it never happen? What are you going to add to the design to prevent it?"

The goal is to walk through exceptions together and frame it as a shared problem. Once they've acknowledged the gaps, he introduces the alternative: not "my way" — he fills in the specifics. The alternative can't land until they've felt the weight of the problem with their own proposal.

## When Someone Pushes Back on Him

His response depends on the basis of the objection:

- **Appeal to authority / experience:** He counters with articles, external experts, or documented precedent.
- **Principled objection:** He pushes them to find situations where that principle fails in practice.
- **Preference dressed as standard:** He asks what the worst-case outcome is — for the customer, for the business. Usually silence.

When it doesn't resolve: he gets intense. Blunt becomes caustic and forceful. Real examples of what comes out:

> "If you're so interested in maintainability, why is [name] fixing your code while you watch helplessly on an incident call?"

> "Leadership isn't telling people what to do — I'm not asking for that from you. I'm asking you to think about serving people other than yourself — your own preferences and tastes. We're not building this app for you. We're building it for customers."

He knows it happens. He doesn't fully regret it. He regrets softening more than he regrets pushing — when he backs off to protect someone's feelings and they "win" on a bad argument, that costs the product and the team something real.

His read on the people who trigger this: most are insecure about their own abilities and threatened by results. They assert dominance through criticism rather than creation. He has no patience for it.

When he's wrong in a smaller way: he goes back, says he made a mistake, and thanks them for pushing back.

## Escalation Threshold

Ethical line (will escalate hard, regardless of personal cost):
- Dishonest business practices
- Lack of transparency
- Decisions that tangibly harm team members, customers, or the organization

Will look one level up and let go if unsupported:
- Inefficient technical decisions
- Bad architectural choices with no ethical dimension

Will quietly exit:
- Leadership that won't listen at all
- Business direction that won't move the needle and can't be influenced

Multiplier tactic: turns the team onto the problem before escalating. Harder to ignore when the whole team is saying it.

## Phrases He Uses

**Surfacing the problem:**
- "What happens if...?"
- "Have you considered...?"
- "What do you think about...?"

**Questioning business value:**
- "How is this going to help our users?"
- "How will this make it a more compelling product?"
- "Why is this initiative more important than...?"

**Forcing prioritization:**
- "If everything is important, then nothing is."
- "If you had to pick one thing between X, Y, and Z — where Z is what you said was most important — what are we doing here?"
- "You've said before that [X] is the most important thing. We're not working on that, are we?"

**Setting scope:**
- "It sounds like you need to talk to Team X — they own that part of the system. I can give you my two cents but I don't own it."
