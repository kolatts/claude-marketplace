## Early life

Grew up in **Upper Arlington, Ohio** — idyllic, safe, sheltered. Rode his bike freely, walked to **The Wellington School**, got into mischief in high school. Spent much of his high school years on the campus of **The Ohio State University**.

Took computer programming in high school — the languages were C++ and Pascal (Microsoft Foundation Classes). Enjoyed it but couldn't build anything fun; the boilerplate killed the creativity. The seed was planted. It just had nowhere to grow yet.

His parents — both Indian immigrants — encouraged him toward medicine from childhood. They said it was because he liked to help people. Underneath that, he suspects, was the Indian cultural preference for status, education, and financial success. He could see himself *getting into* medical school. He could never see himself *practicing*.

From age 15, he worked in laboratory basic science research, which led naturally to **Molecular Genetics** at Ohio State — it fulfilled his premed requirements and matched his lab background. The major was a means to an end he didn't fully believe in yet.

## Medicine

**Case Western Reserve University, Cleveland Clinic Lerner College of Medicine.** He got in. He completed most of his clinical rotations. He left in his fourth year.

What he loved: spending long stretches with patients in the hospital, hearing their stories. The diagnostic process — problem-solving a human, not unlike debugging a system. The instinct for helping people was real.

What wore him down: he didn't fit in socially. The longer he spent in hospitals, the more emotionally and spiritually drained he felt. He was drinking more than he should have been. When he played music, he felt authentic. In the hospital, he felt like he didn't belong.

The specific reasons for leaving are private. What he carries from it: the clinical background became directly useful in healthcare software, and the instinct for helping people never left.

## Deadiron

Formed by accident. He was playing **Guilty Gear** on a friend's couch when **Alex Van Ness** walked in, spotted his "Wisdom in Chains" hoodie, and shouted that he loved the band. They talked music, found the same taste, and Alex asked him on the spot if he wanted to start a band. Alex played bass and taught Sunny how to play with others.

**Mark Rheaume** came on drums. The band played shows. Then Mark introduced them to **Tyler Harvey** on lead guitar — and that's when it took off. Eventually **Tom Walling** replaced Mark on drums, and **Jay Bennett** joined on bass.

- **Genre:** Melodic thrash / melodic death metal. Modern Iron Maiden feel, hardcore punk influence, heavy In Flames influence. Harmonized guitars throughout.
- **Lyrics:** Rust Belt industrial decay, blue-collar workers — not fantasy, not aggression. Observation.
- **His role:** Rhythm guitar. He wrote the foundational riffs — the framework — and the band built on top of them. The song became bigger than what he could have written alone. Exactly how he thinks about software.
- **Still plays along to the album.** Still listens.

The band didn't peak until around 2012, a few years after he left medicine. Those in-between years — finding members, writing songs, working through a difficult period — were formative.

## The gap years (2009–2012)

*Handle with care. Use only in pieces where reinvention, identity, or purpose is the explicit subject.*

Leaving medicine felt like failure — to himself and to his parents. He had over-identified with becoming a doctor since childhood. Losing that identity was destabilizing.

Over time, his perspective shifted. He came to believe that a higher power had a different purpose in store — that if he'd succeeded in medicine, he never would have found music, or software, or his real purpose in creating things. The failure became the opening.

The lesson he carries: he doesn't like being limited to a single role or title. He is a creative and a problem-solver first. "That's not my job" is not a sentence he says.

## Software path

A degree felt too slow. He was already working as a **business analyst** and knew some JavaScript. He needed a path to his first engineering job, not a credential.

**Coder Camps** (no longer exists) — chose it because it was .NET-focused; his cousin had a .NET background, and the job market looked strong. Has since come to genuinely love C#.

**First job: FirstMerit Bank** — worked on **eConnect**, a corporate banking internet banking suite. It was part of the appeal that led Huntington Bank to acquire FirstMerit. There he met **Jason Bowling**, with whom he later co-founded **Bluebox Source** and shipped two mobile games.

**PNC Bank** came next, then a path through several companies to **Arcoro**, where he led engineering. Took an extended leave of absence; when he returned, a senior engineer had stepped into his role and kept projects on track. That was one of his proudest moments as a leader — you become unnecessary.

The early instinct at FirstMerit for automation showed up immediately: the data entry work at his nonprofit job was tedious, so he built reports to read the database and auto-generate mail merge letters — automating the chief function of their manager. The impulse to build the thing that does the boring part never went away.

## Engineering philosophy (use in essays and talks)

- **Code quality doesn't matter that much.** Simplicity and radical obviousness beat elegant architecture nobody understands.
- **The biggest mistake teams make:** Building the wrong thing. Or planning too long instead of building and learning from reality.
- **What he wants to be remembered for:** How much fun people had building software. The joy that comes when the environment is safe enough to take creative risks. Not the code. The atmosphere.
- **Leadership style:** Pacesetting innovator — sets the pace by doing the work, not by delegating it.

## Mentoring philosophy

Legacy is what you made possible. The benchmark is whether the people he mentored can operate without him — and surpass what he could have done alone.
