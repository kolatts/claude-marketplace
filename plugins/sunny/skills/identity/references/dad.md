# Dad

*Personal writing by Sunny Kolattukudy. Use as a voice and tone reference for long-form personal pieces — the rhythm, the specificity, the way emotion lands through concrete detail rather than statement.*

---

My dad hated Christopher Columbus before it became cool. He used to say, "How does someone discover a place where people already live?" Discovery was sacred to him - his passion for science was so deep that he worked relentlessly until he retired at the age of 80. When I asked him why, he said: "When you learn something no one else has ever learned, it's like a secret, it's such a thrill." He had many accomplishments throughout his career but that's not the person I remember the most.

The person I remember told certain stories over and over again. Like the one where he received the call from the OBGYN in Pullman, Washington to let him know that the baby would be a boy - and that he physically jumped up with joy and shouted in victory. I used to think that he had just forgotten how often he told it to me, but now I like to believe that he repeated it to make sure I knew how deeply I was loved even before I was born.

As I went through adulthood, though, I felt like I had disappointed him with many of my choices. I was worried about going down to Florida and introducing Brandy to him, and I remember my sister Angela telling me - "You have to bring the boys! Dad never met a kid that he didn't love completely." And she was right, and I had forgotten that about him - he cherished the boys and came to see Brandy as his own daughter.

When we found out we were expecting Luna and announced it to him, his eyebrows spiked and he slapped his thighs in what can only be described as the thrill of discovery. When she was born, we were in for some surprises. Her right ear was different - and we weren't sure that she'd be able to hear. After just a day, she started having trouble breathing and keeping her oxygen levels up, and finally was admitted to the NICU. Brandy and I were really worried about her, and on top of it, I can't explain why, but I felt like I had let my dad down. Standing outside of Grady Memorial Hospital in Delaware, I called him and said, "Dad, I'm scared. This wasn't supposed to happen. What if she's not okay? Why do I feel like I messed up?" He said in his characteristic blunt style, "Sunnymon, you're absolutely wrong. She is your perfect Kolattukudy princess."

Everyone saw the joy he had, just to be near her. If you look at our pictures you'll see the utter adoration in his eyes. And today, I want you to know that he looked at me the exact same way at that age. Sometimes I feel like I'm ruined forever, because I don't think anyone will ever love me as much as he did.

---

My dad has a big story, and he was a big person. He was born into poverty in a family of farmers in India. He became a school principal at 19. And at 22 years old, he got on a boat and left everything he knew. He traveled through the Suez Canal, through Naples, and finally arrived in New York—alone, with papers that said his final destination was Bozeman, Montana. The immigration official looked at him, looked at the paperwork, and said, "Do you like horses?" At a bus stop in Philadelphia, he realized he needed to eat, but he didn't know what American food was. Someone told him to order a hamburger - which he had never heard of. And he liked it. So through Chicago and on up to Bozeman, at every stop, he ordered a hamburger - the only thing he knew.

That's how his American story started. Instead of horses, in Montana he found love. He built a family and had five children that he loved every day until he died, even if he didn't always show it well. And when his marriage ended, he was completely heartbroken - in a way that took decades to heal. But... then he fell in love again and rediscovered fatherhood with me.

My father discovered America, and he found that it wasn't a place. He discovered ideas, knowledge, potential, and futures - not just for himself but our extended family. He brought my cousins Poulose, Saju, and Jose here from India, where they built their own families and careers woven into the American story forever. And especially now in our nation, this story is an important one to tell.

In the past three years, my father's sharp mind was dulled by dementia and his body slowly hollowed out. As his strength faded, he traded his commanding presence for gratitude and the sweetest kindness. We tried to anchor him with science journals and newspapers—the love language of his life's work. Even his interest for those eventually died out. After a while, the basic pleasures of food and television had lost their luster. It felt like pieces of his life were being peeled off like old paint - like we were getting to the very core of his being. But when everything else was gone, his love for my daughter and I endured. It was all that remained. And that was our greatest discovery.
