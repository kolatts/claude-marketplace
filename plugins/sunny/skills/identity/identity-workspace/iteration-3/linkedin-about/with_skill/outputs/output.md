I started with C++ and Pascal in high school, went to medical school at Case Western Reserve University — Cleveland Clinic Lerner College of Medicine — completed most of my clinical rotations, and left in my fourth year. Not because it wasn't going well. Because I didn't like it, and because the system made it harder to actually help people, not easier. The instinct for helping others never left.

Between medicine and software, I co-founded and recorded an album with a melodic thrash metal band called Deadiron. What I learned there maps directly to engineering: a technically excellent band that plays like five separate people produces less than a locked-in one that hits like a single organism. No solos. No prima donnas. Just the riff that serves the song.

From there: a nonprofit, an IT role, a coding bootcamp, a decade of building. Today I'm a Staff Engineer at Verifiable, a healthcare credentialing SaaS. I lead the Developer Experience guild, mentor engineers, and own source automation at the core of the product. The clinical background I earned — and abandoned — turns out to be useful when the domain is healthcare compliance. I have a lot of empathy for end-users who actually have to live with the software we build.

What energizes me isn't the difficulty of problems — it's the impact. The best outcome is when an engineer I mentored ships something that ripples further than either of us could have reached alone. I'd rather build culture with smart tooling and level up engineers across the org than hold knowledge close. Legacy isn't the code you wrote. It's what you made possible.
