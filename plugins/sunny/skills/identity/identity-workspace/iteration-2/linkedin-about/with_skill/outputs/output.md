I started with C++ and Pascal in high school, went to medical school at Case Western Reserve University's Cleveland Clinic Lerner College of Medicine, completed most of my clinical rotations, and walked away in my fourth year. Not because it wasn't going well — because I didn't like it, and because the system made it harder to help people, not easier.

Between medicine and software, I co-founded a thrash metal band called Deadiron. We recorded an album. What that taught me about building software: a technically excellent team that plays like five separate people produces less than a locked-in one that hits like a single organism. No solos. No prima donnas. Just the riff that serves the song.

From there: a nonprofit, an IT role, a coding bootcamp, a decade of building.

Today I'm a Staff Engineer at Verifiable, a healthcare credentialing SaaS. I mentor engineers, lead our Developer Experience guild, and own the source automation at the core of the product. The clinical background I earned — and left — turns out to be genuinely useful when the domain is healthcare compliance. The empathy for end-users it gave me never went away.

I also consult through my business, Imagile, focused on AI-forward software.

What I care about isn't the difficulty of problems. It's the impact. The best outcome is when the engineer I mentored ships the thing I never could have. That's the whole point.

The books that shaped how I think aren't technical: *Drive*, Lencioni's *Five Dysfunctions*, *The Phoenix Project*. Accountability is forward-looking. Blame is lazy. Priorities are singular by definition.

I'm here to meet people who want to build great things — and people with tough problems to solve.
