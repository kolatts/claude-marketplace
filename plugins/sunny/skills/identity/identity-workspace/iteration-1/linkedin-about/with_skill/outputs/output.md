I started with C++ and Pascal in high school, went to medical school at Case Western / Cleveland Clinic, and left in my fourth year. Not because it wasn't going well — because I realized the system I'd be working inside made it harder to help people, not easier.

Between medicine and software, I co-founded a heavy metal band called Deadiron. We recorded an album. It turns out that building something collaborative — where the output is genuinely greater than the parts — applies equally to software. I didn't know that at the time. I do now.

From there: a nonprofit, an IT role that grew out of necessity, a coding bootcamp, and a decade of building software. Today I'm an IC and technical leader at Verifiable, a healthcare credentialing SaaS. The clinical background I spent years earning turns out to be useful here in ways I didn't expect.

I also run Imagile (imagile.dev), an AI-forward software consulting practice.

What draws me isn't the hardness of a problem. It's what the solution actually does. I'm energized by developer tooling, full-stack product work where you own something end to end, and growing engineers who'll outgrow the team — which is the whole point.

My engineering philosophy isn't complicated: accountability is forward-looking, blame is lazy, and priorities are singular by definition. I've learned as much from bad teams as good ones. Probably more. The books that shaped how I think aren't technical ones — *Drive*, *The Five Dysfunctions*, *The Phoenix Project*.

I build the way I make music: direct, collaborative, with an eye on what the whole thing is doing rather than just my part.

The path here wasn't straight. That stopped bothering me a long time ago.
